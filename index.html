Chapter 01: The Cause

They burn almost nothing.
The world burns everything.

Greenhouse gas emissions per capita reveal a striking disparity. Pacific Island nations are among the world's smallest contributors to climate change — yet they pay the highest price for it.

Chapter 02: Evidence

The ocean is warming.
The rain is shifting.
The sea is rising.

Four physical signals, recorded in data stretching back to 1850, tell the same story: the climate around Pacific Island nations is changing faster than almost anywhere else on Earth.

Why show four signals at once, rather than one? Because each is measured independently — with different instruments, over different time spans. When all four move in the same direction simultaneously, it is no longer coincidence. It is a pattern: the physical evidence behind the tiny emission footprint shown in Chapter 1.

Chapter 04: Long-term Impact

When the harvest fails.
When the water runs dry.

Climate change does not always strike dramatically. It also erodes slowly — through declining crop yields, struggling livestock, and freshwater that grows scarcer every year.

If the previous three chapters explained the cause and the sudden shocks, this chapter shows the slow bleed: the food and water systems that quietly deteriorate day by day.

Twelve nations. One ocean.
Here are every Pacific nation in our dataset, plotted by what they emit and how many people have been affected by climate disasters. At first glance, they look roughly equal.
02 / 05
Most of them barely contribute.
Marshall Islands, Nauru, Tuvalu, and Kiribati release only a fraction of a ton of CO₂ per person each year. Their entire populations could vanish from the global emissions ledger and the numbers would barely shift.
03 / 05
But disasters don't care who is responsible.
Fiji, Solomon Islands, and Vanuatu are hit repeatedly by cyclones and floods. Hundreds of thousands of people are affected, again and again — regardless of how little carbon their countries have ever burned.
04 / 05
Low emissions. High impact. Same countries.
Overlay both perspectives, and a quadrant emerges: nations that have contributed almost nothing to this crisis, yet carry a wildly disproportionate share of the consequences. This is the paradox zone.

Closing Note

This is what the data leaves us with.

Five chapters, one thread: the nations least responsible for climate change bear the heaviest burden from it. Here is what the numbers say.

01
The Cause
Pacific nations emit only a fraction of a ton of CO₂ per person, while the global average approaches 4.7 tons. Their footprint is nearly invisible in the global emissions ledger.
02
The Evidence
Sea surface temperatures, land temperatures, rainfall, and sea levels are all moving in the same direction: up, and faster than before.
03
The Human Cost
More than a million people across the Pacific have been directly affected by disasters. Some nations have lost the equivalent of their entire population in a single year.
04
The Long-term Impact
Crop yields are declining, livestock productivity is weakening, and safe drinking water remains out of reach for many. The damage is slow — but its effects endure.

The Pacific did not create this crisis. Yet its nations are asked to adapt the fastest, lose the most, and speak the loudest just to be heard. If climate justice has any meaning, it begins with acknowledging who is paying the price for a fire they did not start.
